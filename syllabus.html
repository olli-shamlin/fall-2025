Syllabus

Aims and Objectives

The primary aims of this course are to increase the student's digital literacy and digital proficiency and equip/enable the student to better assess their needs relative their knowledge/skills and address them more effectively/strategically. The course's central themes are

  1. The technologies that provide the foundation of everything we do when online: hardware, software, the Internet and the Cloud
  2. Digital data: what it is and how it behaves
  3. The vernacular of digital technology and the vocabulary and devices and apps

The overarching objectives of this course are

  1. To explain how the digital technologies listed work the relationship between the foundational technologies list above and how they work.
  2. To define what digital data is, its characteristics, and its behaviors.
  3. To enhance the student's understanding digital technology terminology.
  4. To orient the student to
    1. The vernacular of software (i.e., the menus, controls, and icons commonly seen on screen).
    2. Digital technology vocabulary (eg, terms and acronyms).
  5. To introduce the student to a framework for describing their digital "lifestyle" across three dimensions: (1) what you do, (2) how you do it (i.e., extent of domain knowledge and skill proficiency) and (3) the benefit(s) you get (i.e., risk vs reward).

Learning Outcomes

Upon successful completion of the course, students will be able to

  1. Describe the similarities and differences between the types of devices (i.e., desktop/personal computers, laptops, tablets, and phone).
  2. Name the key components that are inside all digital devices, describe their purpose.
  3. Enumerate the set of device components that enable how we interact with them, and the common gestures (eg, point, click, select, drag, etc.) we use to "talk to" devices.
  4. Assess the size (ie, power and capacity) of a device based on the size of its components.
  5. Describe the similarities and differences between types of apps (i.e., desktop, mobile, and web).
  6. Identify UI controls (eg, buttons, menus, pickers, sliders, etc.), common icons, and their normal meaning/purpose.
  7. Describe data in terms of type and size and how data is propagated between personal devices and sites/services on the Internet.
  8. Describe the interactions between devices and sites on the Internet when performing actions like sending and receiving email, signing in and out of a site, (like email, browser, social media, search engines, etc.) and consuming content on the internet (e.g., audio/video streaming services like Audible and YouTube.)
  9. Name primary features of the Cloud and when/why using their use can be beneficial.
  10. Differentiate between consumer and commercial clouds. I.e., what it means when a company "moves to the Cloud" and how that is differs from "using the Cloud" from our laptops, tablets, and phones.

Pre-Requisites

This course is designed for general audiences; no preexisting technical knowledge related to computers, software, the internet or the cloud is required.

Materials

Students will not need to purchase any specific materials to fully participate in this course. Any instructor recommended reference and/or reading material will be readily/freely accessible via the Internet and linked to this site.

Session Format

Sessions are 90 minutes in length. Each session will be a combination of lecture and Q&A.

What if I miss a session?

Each session's content builds on content from sessions that precede it. If you miss a session, you will be able to find that session's lecture materials (e.g., slides and any associated references/reading materials provided by the instructor) on this website. You are encouraged to review the material for missed session(s) prior to the next session you attend.
